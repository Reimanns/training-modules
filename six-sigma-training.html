Core Methodology
DMAIC — The Improvement Framework

Every structured improvement follows five phases. Think of DMAIC as a GPS for problem-solving — it keeps you from jumping to solutions before you truly understand the problem.

D
Define
What's the problem? Who does it affect? What's the goal?
M
Measure
How bad is it? What does the data say? Establish baseline.
A
Analyze
Why does it happen? What's the root cause?
I
Improve
What's the fix? Test it, pilot it, validate it.
C
Control
How do we sustain it? Standard work, monitoring, handoff.
Key Principle

Most failed improvements jump straight from Define to Improve — skipping Measure and Analyze entirely. DMAIC forces you to understand the problem before you solve it. Data beats opinion every time.

Module 1 · Section 1
Why Continuous Improvement Matters
The Core Idea

Continuous improvement isn't about working harder — it's about working smarter. It's a systematic approach to finding and eliminating waste so that more of your effort goes toward work that actually matters.

In MRO operations, the stakes are real. Every hour a technician spends waiting for parts, searching for tools, or redoing work is an hour that could have been spent on value-added tasks — the work our customers pay us for. Structured improvement has already proven its value at Citadel Aviation, and there's far more opportunity ahead.

But here's the thing — that number didn't come from one big initiative. It came from dozens of focused improvements across Design, Interiors, Finish Shop, Procurement, and more. The biggest untapped resource in any organization is the knowledge of the people doing the work. That's you.

✦ What CI Is — And What It Isn't
CI IS:
  • → Everyone's responsibility
  • → Data-driven, not opinion-driven
  • → Small, consistent changes over time
  • → Focused on process, not people
  • → About making your job easier
CI IS NOT:
  • → Blame or punishment
  • → Working faster or harder
  • → A one-time event
  • → Just for engineers or management
  • → About cutting headcount
Citadel's Improvement Principles

1. Respect for People — The people closest to the work know the most about it.
2. Go and See (Gemba) — Observe the work where it happens, not from a conference room.
3. Data Over Opinion — Measure before you change. Validate after.
4. Small Steps, Big Impact — Consistent 1% improvements compound into transformation.
5. Standardize Before You Optimize — You can't improve chaos.
6. Make It Stick — An improvement that reverts isn't an improvement.

Module 1 · Section 3
Types of Improvements

Not every improvement needs a full project. At Citadel, we classify improvements into four types based on complexity and scope. Knowing which type you're dealing with helps you choose the right approach.

A Just Do It LOW COMPLEXITY

Simple fixes you can implement yourself or with your team. No formal project needed — just do it and log it. Examples: reorganizing a toolbox, creating a checklist, labeling storage bins.

B Small Project MODERATE

Requires some data collection and analysis. Typically 2-6 weeks. Cross-functional coordination may be needed. This is the standard improvement project level. Examples: reducing rework in a specific process, improving parts delivery time.

C Major Initiative HIGH COMPLEXITY

Full DMAIC project. Multiple departments, significant data analysis, formal project charter. Led by OpEx with cross-functional team. Examples: hangar workflow redesign, digital transformation initiatives.

D Strategic / Capital ENTERPRISE

Requires capital investment, executive sponsorship, and long-term planning. Often multi-phase with significant organizational change. Examples: new facility layout, ERP implementation, mobile technician tablet program.

Your Role

At the awareness level, focus on identifying Type A opportunities and submitting ideas for Type B-D through the OpEx Hub. As you build skills, you'll lead Type B projects independently, with coaching from OpEx. The goal is to build a pipeline — the more Type A and B improvements the org can handle without OpEx involvement, the more capacity we have for the big stuff.

Module 2 · Section 1
Writing Good Problem Statements
Why This Matters

A well-written problem statement is the single most important deliverable in the Define phase. If you can't clearly articulate the problem, you can't solve it — and you definitely can't get others to help you solve it.

A good problem statement answers four questions: What is happening? Where is it happening? When did it start or when does it occur? How big is the impact?

❌ Bad Problem Statement

"Parts are always late and it's slowing everything down."

Vague, emotional, no data, implies blame, uses "always."

✓ Good Problem Statement

"In Hangar 2, 37% of interior completions parts requests took longer than 4 hours to fulfill in Q4 2025, contributing to an estimated 120 technician-hours of idle time per month."

Specific location, measurable data, time period, quantified impact.

✦ Problem Statement Formula
In [where], [what] is occurring
since/during [when],
resulting in [magnitude/impact].

Module 2 · Section 3
Gemba Walks — Go and See
What Is Gemba?

"Gemba" is a Japanese word meaning "the real place." In improvement work, it means going to where the work actually happens — the hangar floor, the receiving dock, the finish shop — and observing with fresh eyes. You can't improve what you don't understand, and you can't understand what you haven't seen.

A Gemba walk isn't a tour and it's not an audit. It's a structured observation to understand how work actually flows — not how it's supposed to flow according to the SOP. The gap between those two things is where improvement lives.

✦ Gemba Walk Protocol
01
Prepare
Define what you're observing. Bring a notebook, not a clipboard. Tell the team you're there to learn, not to judge.
02
Observe
Watch the flow of work. Note waste categories (DOWNTIME). Time activities. Watch for workarounds. Don't interrupt unless invited.
03
Ask
Ask "what makes your job difficult?" and "if you could change one thing, what would it be?" Listen more than you talk.
04
Document
Record observations factually. Separate what you saw from what you think it means. Note timestamps, locations, and counts.
05
Follow Up
Share what you learned with the team. Acknowledge their input. Close the loop on any quick fixes. Build trust for the next visit.
Golden Rule

Never walk the Gemba and leave without acknowledging the people. If someone took time to explain their work to you, follow up. Nothing kills improvement culture faster than people feeling like they shared problems that went into a black hole.

Module 3 · Section 1
5-Whys — Digging to the Root
Core Principle

Most people solve symptoms, not problems. The 5-Whys technique forces you to keep asking "why?" until you get past the surface and reach the root cause — the thing that, if fixed, prevents the problem from recurring.

The technique is deceptively simple: start with the problem, ask "why does this happen?", take that answer, and ask "why?" again. Repeat until you reach a cause you can actually act on. Five is a guideline, not a rule — sometimes you hit root cause at 3, sometimes at 7.

✈️ MRO Example — Walkthrough
PROBLEM
Aircraft delivery was delayed by 3 days.
WHY 1
Why was delivery delayed?
Interior seat installation took 2 extra days due to rework.
WHY 2
Why did seat installation require rework?
Wrong mounting hardware was used on 12 seat tracks.
WHY 3
Why was wrong hardware used?
The parts kit contained NAS bolts instead of the specified AN bolts.
WHY 4
Why did the kit contain the wrong bolts?
The kitting technician pulled from an unlabeled bin that contained both NAS and AN bolts mixed together.
WHY 5
Why were different bolts mixed in the same bin?
There is no standard for hardware bin labeling or separation in the kit room, and no verification step in the kitting process.
ROOT CAUSE IDENTIFIED
No standard for hardware bin organization + no verification step in kitting. The fix isn't "tell the tech to be more careful" — it's establishing bin labeling standards and adding a visual verification check to the kitting process.
⚠️ Common 5-Why Traps
×
Stopping Too Early
"Because the tech made a mistake" is not a root cause. Why did the process allow the mistake?
×
Blaming People Instead of Process
If your root cause is a person's name, keep asking. The system should prevent errors, not rely on perfection.
×
Following Only One Branch
Problems often have multiple contributing causes. Branch your "whys" when you see more than one valid answer.
×
No Data to Support
Each "why" should be validated with evidence. If you're guessing, you need to go collect data before continuing.

Module 3 · Section 2
Fishbone Diagrams (Ishikawa)

A fishbone diagram (also called Ishikawa or cause-and-effect diagram) organizes potential causes of a problem into categories. It's especially powerful when a 5-Why analysis branches into multiple directions, or when you're working with a team to brainstorm all possible contributing factors.

The 6Ms Framework

In manufacturing and MRO, we organize causes into 6 categories — the 6Ms. Not every problem will have causes in all six, but checking each category ensures you don't miss anything.

M1
Manpower
Training, experience, staffing levels, fatigue, skill gaps, communication between team members
M2
Methods
Work instructions, procedures, process design, SOPs, standard work, sequence of operations
M3
Machines
Equipment, tools, software, calibration, maintenance, availability, technology limitations
M4
Materials
Raw materials, parts quality, availability, specifications, shelf life, storage conditions
M5
Measurement
Inspection criteria, gauges, data accuracy, how we define "good," measurement methods
M6
Mother Nature
Environment, temperature, humidity, lighting, workspace layout, physical conditions
✦ How to Build a Fishbone in Practice
01
Write the problem on the right
This is the "head" of the fish. Use your problem statement from Module 2.
02
Draw the 6M categories as "bones"
Branches off the main spine. Label each: Manpower, Methods, Machines, Materials, Measurement, Mother Nature.
03
Brainstorm causes under each M
Work with your team. Every idea goes up — no filtering during brainstorming. Use sticky notes on a whiteboard.
04
Prioritize with data
After brainstorming, vote or use data to identify the most likely root causes. Circle the top 2-3. Then validate with evidence.
When to Use Which Tool

Use 5-Whys when the problem is straightforward, you have a clear starting point, and you're working alone or with a small group. Use a Fishbone when the problem is complex, has many potential causes, and you need a cross-functional team to brainstorm systematically. Often they work together — fishbone to generate possibilities, then 5-Whys to drill into the top suspects.

Module 3 · Section 3
Data-Driven Decisions
The Golden Rule of Analysis

In improvement work, data beats opinion every time. "I think" gets you a conversation. "The data shows" gets you a decision. Your job isn't to guess what's wrong — it's to prove it.

At this level, you don't need advanced statistics. You need to understand the difference between process metrics and outcome metrics, how to collect clean data, and how to present findings so leadership can make decisions.

Process Metrics (Leading)

Measure the inputs and activities — things you can influence in real time.

  • → Parts request fulfillment time
  • → Technician utilization rate per shift
  • → First-pass inspection rate
  • → Kit accuracy percentage
Outcome Metrics (Lagging)

Measure the results — tell you how things turned out after the fact.

  • → On-time delivery rate
  • → Total rework hours per aircraft
  • → Customer satisfaction scores
  • → Cost per aircraft completion
✦ Data Collection Best Practices
✓Define exactly what you're measuring before you start collecting
✓Use tally sheets, check sheets, or simple forms — not memory
✓Collect at consistent times, using consistent methods
✓Record who collected the data, when, and how
✓Pareto chart your findings: 80% of problems usually come from 20% of causes
×Don't collect data for the sake of collecting data — know why each metric matters
×Don't change your measurement method mid-study
Pareto Principle

The Pareto chart is your best friend at this level. It ranks causes by frequency or impact, showing where to focus. If 3 out of 15 defect types account for 78% of your rework hours, those 3 are your improvement targets — not all 15. Prioritize ruthlessly.

Module 4 · Section 1
Generating & Selecting Solutions
Key Mindset Shift

If you've done Modules 1-3 well, solution generation should feel almost obvious. The hard work is in the analysis. At this point you know the root cause, you have data, and you've scoped the problem. Now you need to find the right fix — not just any fix.

The biggest mistake at the Improve phase is jumping to the first solution that comes to mind. Instead, generate multiple options, evaluate them against criteria, and select the one with the best combination of impact, feasibility, and sustainability.

✦ Impact-Effort Matrix
🏆 Quick Wins
HIGH IMPACT · LOW EFFORT
Do these first. Easy to implement with significant results. Build momentum and credibility.
📋 Major Projects
HIGH IMPACT · HIGH EFFORT
Worth doing, but requires planning, resources, and executive sponsorship. Phase these in.
🔧 Fill-Ins
LOW IMPACT · LOW EFFORT
Nice to have. Do these when you have spare cycles, but don't prioritize over bigger wins.
⛔ Time Sinks
LOW IMPACT · HIGH EFFORT
Avoid. These consume resources without meaningful return. Don't let passion projects live here.
✦ Solution Evaluation Criteria

Score each potential solution 1-5 on these dimensions. The highest total score wins.

IMPACTHow much will this reduce or eliminate the root cause?
FEASIBILITYCan we actually implement this with available resources and authority?
COSTWhat's the investment required? (Time, money, materials)
SUSTAINABILITYWill this stick without constant monitoring? Is it self-reinforcing?
SPEEDHow quickly can we implement and see results?

Module 4 · Section 2
Piloting & Testing Changes
Why Pilot First

Never roll out a change across the entire operation at once. A pilot lets you test your solution on a small scale, catch unintended consequences, refine the approach, and build evidence that it works — all before you ask for full commitment from leadership and the team.

Think of a pilot as a controlled experiment. You're testing a hypothesis: "If we implement [solution X], then [root cause Y] will be reduced by [amount Z]." The pilot gives you the data to prove or disprove that hypothesis before going all-in.

🔄 PDCA — The Pilot Cycle
P
Plan
Define what you'll test, where, for how long, what you'll measure, and what success looks like. Write it down — a pilot with no plan is just winging it.
D
Do
Execute the pilot. Implement the change on the defined scope. Collect data throughout. Document what happens — expected and unexpected.
C
Check
Compare results to your plan. Did the metric improve? By how much? Were there side effects? What did the team say? Data + feedback = full picture.
A
Act
If it worked: standardize and expand. If it partially worked: adjust and re-pilot. If it didn't work: learn why and try a different approach. Every outcome is data.
✈️ Pilot Plan Template
WhatWhat change are we testing?
WhereWhich area, line, shift, or team?
DurationHow long will the pilot run? (Usually 1-4 weeks)
MetricWhat will we measure? What's the baseline? What's the target?
Success CriteriaHow will we know if it worked?
Rollback PlanIf it fails, how do we revert safely?

Module 4 · Section 3
Leading Change — The Human Side
The Hard Truth

Most improvements don't fail because of bad analysis or wrong solutions. They fail because people resist the change. A technically perfect solution that nobody adopts is a waste of everyone's time. Change management is how you make improvements stick.

People don't resist change itself — they resist being changed without being consulted. The number one predictor of whether an improvement will stick is whether the people affected by it were involved in creating it. That's not just good management — it's the entire foundation of the Lean principle "Respect for People."

01 Involve Early, Not Late

Bring the people who will be affected by the change into the process during the Analyze phase — not after you've already decided on a solution. When people help build the solution, they own it.

02 Explain the Why

People need to understand why the change matters — to them, to the team, and to the business. "Because OpEx said so" is not a reason. Connect the improvement to their daily pain points.

03 Start with Champions

Find 1-2 people on the team who are natural early adopters. Get them on board first. Peer influence is 10x more powerful than top-down mandates. Let them demonstrate success.

04 Listen to Resistance

Resistance often carries valuable information. "That won't work because..." might reveal a constraint you missed. Don't dismiss pushback — investigate it. Some of the best solutions come from objections.

05 Show Results Fast

Nothing wins converts like visible success. Share early pilot data. Celebrate quick wins publicly. The faster people can see that the change works, the faster resistance melts away.

The Adoption Curve

You don't need 100% buy-in to start. In most teams, ~15% are early adopters, ~60% are in the middle (waiting to see if it works), and ~25% will resist until they have no choice. Your strategy: win the 15%, use their success to pull the 60%, and the 25% will follow when the new way becomes the norm.

Module 5 · Section 1
Standard Work — The Baseline for Improvement
Why Standard Work Exists

You can't improve chaos. Standard work documents the current best-known way to perform a task — the sequence, timing, and key quality points. It's not about rigid rules. It's about creating a stable baseline so that when things go wrong, you can identify what changed.

Here's the critical distinction: standard work is the floor, not the ceiling. It captures today's best practice so that tomorrow's improvement has something to build on. Without it, every shift does things differently, and "improvement" is just noise on top of variation.

Good Standard Work
  • → Written by the people who do the work
  • → Includes photos, visuals, and key quality checks
  • → Lives at the point of use (not in a binder on a shelf)
  • → Updated when a better method is proven
  • → Trained and verified through observation
Bad Standard Work
  • → Written by someone who doesn't do the work
  • → Walls of text with no visuals
  • → Buried in a shared drive nobody checks
  • → Last updated 3 years ago
  • → Created for compliance, not for use
✦ Standard Work Elements
SEQUENCEThe exact order of steps to complete the task
TIMEExpected duration for each step and the total task
KEY POINTSCritical quality, safety, or technique notes at each step
MATERIALSTools, parts, and consumables needed before starting
VISUALSPhotos of correct vs. incorrect, diagrams, annotated images

Module 5 · Section 2
Visual Management — Make Status Obvious
The Principle

A well-managed workplace tells you its status at a glance. Visual management means designing the work environment so that anyone — a tech, a supervisor, or a visitor — can immediately see what's normal, what's abnormal, and what needs attention. No digging through systems, no asking around.

The best visual systems are so intuitive that they require zero training to understand. Think traffic lights: red means stop, green means go. Nobody reads a manual for that. Your workplace visuals should work the same way.

🏷️
Labels & Signs
Every bin, tool, and location clearly identified
📋
Status Boards
Daily metrics, work status, blockers visible to all
🔲
Shadow Boards
Outlined tool holders — missing items immediately obvious
🚦
Color Coding
Red/yellow/green for status, zones, safety
📊
Min/Max Indicators
Inventory levels visible — reorder when below the line
📐
Floor Markings
Designated areas for WIP, staging, paths, safety zones
✦ 5S — The Foundation of Visual Management
S1
Sort
Remove everything that isn't needed for the current work. If you haven't used it in 30 days, red-tag it.
S2
Set in Order
A place for everything, everything in its place. Organize by frequency of use — most used items closest to hand.
S3
Shine
Clean the work area regularly. Cleaning is inspection — you notice leaks, damage, and wear when you clean.
S4
Standardize
Create visual standards so everyone maintains the same system. Photo references of "what right looks like."
S5
Sustain
Make it habit. Audits, checklists, leadership walks. The system works only if it's maintained daily, not just after a cleanup event.

Module 5 · Section 3
Control Plans — Making Improvements Stick
The Uncomfortable Truth

Most improvements revert within 6 months. Not because the solution was wrong, but because nobody built a system to sustain it. A control plan is that system — it defines what to monitor, how often, by whom, and what to do when things drift.

Think of a control plan as the immune system for your improvement. It detects when the process starts drifting back toward the old way and triggers a response before you lose the gains. Without it, entropy wins every time.

✦ Control Plan Template
What to MonitorThe key metric or condition that tells you the improvement is holding
Target / SpecWhat "good" looks like — the range of acceptable performance
FrequencyHow often you check — daily, weekly, per batch, per shift
Who ChecksSpecific role or person responsible for monitoring
How to CheckThe method — visual inspection, data pull, checklist, audit
Response PlanIf out of spec: who to notify, what corrective action to take, escalation path
Signs It's Working
→ Metric stays in range consistently
→ Process owner checks without being reminded
→ Team self-corrects when drift is noticed
→ New hires are trained on the improved way
Signs It's Failing
→ Nobody remembers who's supposed to check
→ Data collection has stopped
→ Old habits creeping back on certain shifts
→ No one has looked at the control plan in weeks
Handoff is Everything

The final step of any improvement project is the formal handoff to the process owner — the person who lives with this process every day. They need to own the control plan, not OpEx. If the process owner can't explain the control plan from memory, the handoff isn't done.

The Project Charter — Your Contract
Your Milestone

This is where it gets real. You'll lead a Type B improvement project from start to finish — Define through Control. The project charter is your starting document. It aligns you, your team, your sponsor, and OpEx on exactly what you're doing and why.

Think of the charter as a contract between you and the organization. It says: "Here's the problem I'm solving, here's my scope, here's what success looks like, and here are the resources I need." It protects you from scope creep and gives leadership confidence that you have a plan.

✦ Charter Template
Problem Statement
What's happening, where, since when, and what's the impact? (Use your Module 2 skills)
Goal Statement
What metric will improve, by how much, by when? Make it SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.
Scope
What's in scope and what's explicitly out of scope? Include SIPOC. Define boundaries clearly to prevent creep.
Team & Stakeholders
Project lead (you), sponsor (executive), process owner, team members, and their roles. Who needs to approve changes?
Timeline
Target milestones for each DMAIC phase. Total project duration: typically 8-12 weeks for Type B.
Expected Impact
Estimated savings in time, cost, quality, or capacity. This doesn't have to be exact yet — refine as you collect data.

Module 6 · Section 2
Executing Your Improvement Project
Your Roadmap

Here's the truth about improvement projects: they're not supposed to be perfect. They're supposed to be real. You will hit obstacles, your data won't be clean, and your first solution idea might not work. That's normal. The methodology exists to guide you through those moments, not to eliminate them.

D Define (Week 1-2)

Finalize your charter. Get sponsor sign-off. Build your SIPOC. Present the problem to your team and get their input. This phase ends when everyone agrees on what you're solving.

Deliverable: Approved charter + SIPOC
M Measure (Week 2-4)

Collect your baseline data. Map the process in detail. Quantify the problem — frequency, duration, cost. Go to the Gemba. This phase ends when you can say "here's exactly how bad it is, proven by data."

Deliverable: Baseline data + detailed process map
A Analyze (Week 4-6)

Run your 5-Whys and fishbone analysis. Identify the root cause(s). Validate with data — don't just brainstorm, prove it. This phase ends when you can say "the root cause is X, and here's the evidence."

Deliverable: Root cause analysis + validation data
I Improve (Week 6-9)

Generate solutions, select the best one, pilot it. Collect data during the pilot. Compare to baseline. Refine if needed. This phase ends when you have proven results from a controlled pilot.

Deliverable: Pilot results + before/after comparison
C Control (Week 9-12)

Standardize the solution. Write or update standard work. Create the control plan. Train the team. Hand off to the process owner. Document lessons learned. This phase ends when the process owner can sustain the improvement without you.

Deliverable: Standard work + control plan + lessons learned
OpEx Coaching

You won't be doing this alone. OpEx will coach you through each phase — reviewing your deliverables, helping you unstick when you're stuck, and ensuring your methodology is sound. Think of it as having a guide on the trail. You're doing the climbing, but you won't get lost.

Presenting Your Results
The Final Gate

Your certification culminates in a presentation to leadership and the OpEx team. This isn't just a formality — it's where you demonstrate that you can take a real business problem through the full DMAIC cycle and deliver measurable results. It's also your chance to get recognized for the impact you've created.

Your presentation should tell a story: "Here was the problem. Here's how bad it was. Here's what caused it. Here's what we did. Here's what happened. Here's how it stays." If you can tell that story clearly, backed by data, you've demonstrated the skills of a trained CI practitioner.

✦ Presentation Structure (15-20 min)
1
Problem & Business Case
2-3 min. Problem statement, why it matters, who's affected, cost of doing nothing.
2
Baseline Data & Measurement
3 min. What you measured, how, and what the data showed. Visual charts preferred.
3
Root Cause Analysis
3 min. Show your 5-Why or fishbone. Walk through the logic. Show how data validated the root cause.
4
Solution & Pilot Results
4 min. What you implemented, the pilot design, before/after comparison, statistical or visual proof of improvement.
5
Control & Sustainability
2 min. Your control plan, standard work updates, process owner handoff, monitoring approach.
6
Impact & Lessons Learned
2 min. Total quantified impact (time, cost, quality). What went well, what you'd do differently, what surprised you.
